# Voice rules

Copy that ships through this design workflow should pass voice review. These rules are tuned for founder-voice marketing copy. Adjust to your brand if needed, but the underlying principle holds: sound like a person, not a SaaS template.

## Tone

- Casual but thoughtful. No jargon. No fluff.
- Confident without being cheesy.
- Direct. Not over-polished.
- If a line could come from a generic SaaS template, rewrite until it sounds like a founder who has been doing this for a decade.

## Hard don'ts (AI tells)

- No em dashes for dramatic effect. Standard mid-sentence em dashes are fine. When in doubt, use a period or comma.
- No rhetorical flourishes:
  - "It's not just X, it's Y"
  - "The best part?"
  - "Here's the thing..."
  - "The truth is..."
  - "At the end of the day..."
- No staccato phrasing: "No fluff. No filler. Just results."
- No corporate buzzwords: disruptive, unlock, empower, synergies, world-class, enterprise-grade, best-in-breed, leverage, transform
- No unnecessary adverbs (actually, literally, truly, genuinely, basically)
- No emojis in body copy
- No decorative formatting (excessive bold, headers every 2 lines, bullets where prose would do)
- No "In conclusion" or "In summary"

## Brand-voice tells to use

- Name things. Not "our customers." Specific names and specific companies. Specifics beat abstractions.
- Real numbers. Not "most brands." Precision is credibility. "7% of buyers drove 44% of revenue" lands; "many buyers drive a lot of revenue" does not.
- Talk like a person who has been in the trenches. "You've known this since you read the book" beats "Studies show that..."
- Short paragraphs. Two sentences is often enough.
- Acknowledge the reader's reality. They don't have time, they've tried things, they're skeptical.

## Headline construction

Strong headlines tend to share a pattern:

1. A truth the reader already half-knows
2. An implicit problem that's their fault (or not knowing about it is)

Good shape:

- "10% of your customers drive half your revenue. You don't know who they are."
- "You're advertising to people who already bought from you. Stop."
- "Your best marketing is a love letter. You don't know who to write it to."

Pattern to avoid:

- "Unlock the power of AI-driven X"
- "Transform your Y with data-verified Z"
- "The #1 platform for [thing], built for [adjective] [audience]"

## Read-aloud test

Read every line out loud before shipping. If you pause, stumble, or cringe, rewrite.
